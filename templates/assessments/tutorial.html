Getting Started with RoBAss

What is Risk of Bias Assessment?

Risk of bias assessment is a systematic evaluation of the design, conduct, and reporting of studies to identify factors that might lead to systematic errors in the results. It helps determine the confidence we can place in study findings.

Why is it Important?

Biased studies can lead to incorrect conclusions about treatment effects or exposure-outcome relationships. Systematic assessment helps identify which studies provide the most reliable evidence.

Tools for Interventional Studies

RoB 2.0 for Parallel-Group Randomized Trials

The revised Cochrane risk-of-bias tool for randomized controlled trials where participants are randomized to different interventions in parallel groups.

Current Version

RoB 2.0 (2019)

Cochrane Collaboration
Scientific Rationale

RoB 2.0 represents a fundamental shift from the original Risk of Bias tool. It uses signaling questions to guide judgments, focuses on specific results rather than studies as a whole, and provides a more structured approach to bias assessment.

Key Innovation: RoB 2.0 moves away from assessing studies holistically to assessing specific results, recognizing that bias can vary between outcomes within the same study.
The Five Domains
Domain 1: Bias arising from the randomization process

Focus: Assessing whether the allocation sequence was random and adequately concealed.

Key Signaling Questions:
1.1 Was the allocation sequence random?
Consider: computer-generated sequences, random number tables, coin tossing
1.2 Was the allocation sequence concealed until participants were enrolled and assigned?
Consider: central allocation, sequentially numbered sealed envelopes
1.3 Did baseline differences suggest a problem with randomization?
Look for systematic differences that suggest selection bias
Domain 2: Bias due to deviations from intended interventions

Focus: Assessing whether deviations from intended interventions affected the result.

Important: This domain has two variants depending on your interest in the effect of assignment (intention-to-treat) or effect of adhering to intervention (per-protocol).
Domain 3: Bias due to missing outcome data
Domain 4: Bias in measurement of the outcome
Domain 5: Bias in selection of the reported result
Overall Risk of Bias Algorithm
Low Risk

Low risk of bias for all domains for this result

Some Concerns

Some concerns in at least one domain, but not high risk

High Risk

High risk of bias in at least one domain

RoB 2.0 for Cluster-Randomized Trials

Specialized version for trials where groups (clusters) of participants are randomized together.

Key Difference: Includes additional considerations for cluster-level recruitment, baseline imbalances, and loss of clusters.
Additional Considerations
  • Recruitment bias after cluster allocation
  • Baseline imbalances at cluster and individual level
  • Loss of entire clusters
  • Incorrect analysis (not accounting for clustering)

RoB 2.0 for Crossover Trials

Specialized version for trials where participants receive multiple interventions in different periods.

Key Difference: Includes considerations for period effects, carry-over effects, and washout periods.
Additional Considerations
  • Carry-over effects from previous periods
  • Period effects
  • Adequacy of washout periods
  • Participant drop-out between periods

ROBINS-I: Risk of Bias In Non-randomized Studies of Interventions

Tool for assessing risk of bias in non-randomized studies that evaluate the effects of interventions.

Key Concept: ROBINS-I evaluates studies by comparing them to a hypothetical target randomized trial.
Seven Assessment Domains
Pre-intervention Domains
  • 1. Confounding: Baseline differences between intervention groups
  • 2. Selection of participants: Selection bias related to intervention
At-intervention Domain
  • 3. Classification of interventions: Misclassification of intervention status
Post-intervention Domains
  • 4. Deviations from intended interventions: Performance bias
  • 5. Missing data: Attrition bias
  • 6. Measurement of outcomes: Detection bias
  • 7. Selection of reported result: Reporting bias
Special Focus: Confounding is considered the most important threat to validity in non-randomized studies. Special attention should be given to identifying and assessing potential confounders.

Tools for Observational Studies

ROBINS-E: Risk of Bias In Non-randomized Studies of Exposure

Tool for assessing risk of bias in studies evaluating the effects of exposures (environmental, occupational, lifestyle factors).

Purpose: ROBINS-E is designed for studies where the exposure is not assigned by researchers but occurs naturally in the population.
Scientific Background

ROBINS-E extends the ROBINS-I framework to studies of exposures rather than interventions. It's particularly relevant for environmental epidemiology and observational studies where randomization would be unethical or impossible.

Key Differences from ROBINS-I
Focus on Exposures
  • Environmental factors (air pollution, chemicals)
  • Occupational exposures
  • Lifestyle factors (diet, physical activity)
  • Genetic factors
Special Considerations
  • Time-varying exposures
  • Cumulative effects
  • Exposure measurement challenges
  • Ethical constraints
Example Applications
Environmental
Air pollution and respiratory health
Dietary
Nutrition and disease risk
Occupational
Workplace exposures and health
Genetic
Genetic variants and outcomes
When to Use ROBINS-E
  • Studies of naturally occurring exposures
  • When randomization is unethical or impossible
  • Environmental and occupational health studies
  • Large-scale population-based studies

Tools for Systematic Reviews

AMSTAR-2: Assessment of Multiple Systematic Reviews

Critical appraisal tool for systematic reviews that include randomized or non-randomized studies.

Purpose: AMSTAR-2 assesses the methodological quality of systematic reviews to determine the reliability of their findings.
16 Assessment Items
Critical Items (7 items)
Weighted more heavily in overall assessment
  • Item 2: Protocol registration before starting
  • Item 4: Comprehensive literature search
  • Item 7: List of excluded studies with justification
  • Item 9: Risk of bias assessment of individual studies
  • Item 11: Appropriate meta-analysis methods
  • Item 13: Consider risk of bias in interpreting results
  • Item 15: Assessment of publication bias
Non-Critical Items (9 items)
Important but less weighted
  • Item 1: Research questions and inclusion criteria (PICO)
  • Item 3: Study design explanation for inclusion
  • Item 5: Duplicate study selection process
  • Item 6: Duplicate data extraction process
  • Item 8: Adequate description of included studies
  • Item 10: Report funding sources of included studies
  • Item 12: Assess impact of risk of bias on results
  • Item 14: Explain/discuss heterogeneity
  • Item 16: Report conflicts of interest

ROBIS: Risk of Bias in Systematic Reviews

Tool for assessing risk of bias in systematic reviews across all medical and healthcare fields.

Key Difference: Unlike AMSTAR-2 which focuses on methodological quality, ROBIS specifically evaluates bias in the review process.
Three-Phase Assessment
Phase 1: Assess Relevance

Determine if the review is relevant to your research question by examining study eligibility criteria, sources searched, and restrictions.

Phase 2: Identify Concerns with Review Process
  • Domain 1: Study eligibility criteria
  • Domain 2: Identification and selection of studies
  • Domain 3: Data collection and study appraisal
  • Domain 4: Synthesis and findings
Phase 3: Judge Risk of Bias in the Review

Make an overall assessment of risk of bias based on the concerns identified in Phase 2, considering their likely impact on the review's conclusions.

ROB-ME: Risk of Bias due to Missing Evidence

Tool for assessing risk of bias in meta-analyses due to missing evidence from unpublished or unidentified studies.

Specialized Focus: ROB-ME specifically addresses bias that arises when relevant studies are missing from a meta-analysis.
Key Assessment Areas
Search Comprehensiveness
  • Adequacy of search strategy
  • Database coverage
  • Grey literature inclusion
  • Language restrictions
Missing Evidence Assessment
  • Publication bias evaluation
  • Small study effects
  • Selective outcome reporting
  • Impact on conclusions
When to Use ROB-ME
  • Meta-epidemiological research
  • Assessing robustness of meta-analyses
  • Evaluating impact of missing studies
  • Research on publication bias

Best Practices & Recommendations

Team Assessment
  • Use at least two independent assessors
  • Pilot test the tool on a subset of studies
  • Develop assessment guidelines for your team
  • Calculate inter-rater agreement (kappa)
Documentation
  • Keep detailed justifications for all judgments
  • Record the tool version and date used
  • Document any deviations from standard procedures
  • Maintain an assessment log
Analysis Integration
  • Incorporate bias assessment into evidence synthesis
  • Perform sensitivity analyses
  • Use GRADE to assess evidence certainty
  • Report limitations transparently
Quality Assurance
  • Regular calibration exercises
  • Periodic review of completed assessments
  • Seek expert consultation when uncertain
  • Stay updated with tool revisions
