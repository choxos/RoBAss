Getting Started with RoBAss

What is Risk of Bias Assessment?

Risk of bias assessment is a systematic evaluation of the design, conduct, and reporting of studies to identify factors that might lead to systematic errors in the results. It helps determine the confidence we can place in study findings.

Why is it Important?

Biased studies can lead to incorrect conclusions about treatment effects or exposure-outcome relationships. Systematic assessment helps identify which studies provide the most reliable evidence.

Tools for Interventional Studies

RoB 2.0 for Parallel-Group Randomized Trials

The revised Cochrane risk-of-bias tool for randomized controlled trials where participants are randomized to different interventions in parallel groups.

Current Version

RoB 2.0 (2019)

Cochrane Collaboration
Scientific Rationale

RoB 2.0 represents a fundamental shift from the original Risk of Bias tool. It uses signaling questions to guide judgments, focuses on specific results rather than studies as a whole, and provides a more structured approach to bias assessment. The tool is based on the principle that bias should be assessed for specific outcomes and at specific time points.

Key Innovation: RoB 2.0 moves away from assessing studies holistically to assessing specific results, recognizing that bias can vary between outcomes within the same study.
The Five Domains Explained
Domain 1: Bias arising from the randomization process

Focus: Assessing whether the allocation sequence was random and adequately concealed.

Key Signaling Questions:
1.1 Was the allocation sequence random?
Consider: computer-generated sequences, random number tables, coin tossing
1.2 Was the allocation sequence concealed until participants were enrolled and assigned?
Consider: central allocation, sequentially numbered sealed envelopes
1.3 Did baseline differences suggest a problem with randomization?
Look for systematic differences that suggest selection bias
Common Problems:
  • Quasi-random methods (alternation, birth dates)
  • Open allocation (unsealed envelopes, predictable sequences)
  • Significant baseline imbalances in key prognostic factors
Domain 2: Bias due to deviations from intended interventions

Focus: Assessing whether deviations from intended interventions affected the result.

Important: This domain has two variants depending on your interest in the effect of assignment (intention-to-treat) or effect of adhering to intervention (per-protocol).
Key Signaling Questions (ITT Effect):
2.1 Were participants aware of their assigned intervention?
Consider blinding of participants
2.2 Were carers and people delivering interventions aware?
Consider blinding of care providers
2.3 Were there deviations from intended interventions that arose because of the trial context?
Deviations that would not occur in real-world settings
Decision Tree:
If blinding was not possible:
Focus on whether lack of blinding led to deviations in care or behavior
If blinding failed:
Assess whether unblinding led to differential care or behavior
Domain 3: Bias due to missing outcome data

Focus: Assessing whether missing outcome data could have affected the result.

Key Signaling Questions:
3.1 Were data for this outcome available for all, or nearly all, participants randomized?
3.2 If N/PN to 3.1: Is there evidence that result was not biased by missing outcome data?
3.3 If N/PN to 3.2: Could missingness depend on the true value?
3.4 If Y/PY to 3.3: Is it likely that missingness depended on the true value?
Missing Data Assessment Guide
Low Risk
≤5% missing data or proper ITT analysis with appropriate methods
Some Concerns
5-20% missing data with unclear handling or balanced missingness
High Risk
>20% missing or differential missingness likely related to outcome
Domain 4: Bias in measurement of the outcome

Focus: Assessing whether outcome measurement was influenced by knowledge of intervention received.

Key Signaling Questions:
4.1 Was the method of measuring the outcome inappropriate?
4.2 Could measurement or ascertainment of the outcome have differed between intervention groups?
4.3 Were outcome assessors aware of the intervention received by study participants?
4.4 If Y/PY to 4.3: Could assessment have been influenced by knowledge of intervention?
Objective Outcomes
  • Death
  • Laboratory values
  • Radiological findings
Usually low risk even without blinding
Subjective Outcomes
  • Pain scores
  • Quality of life
  • Functional assessments
Higher risk if assessors not blinded
Domain 5: Bias in selection of the reported result

Focus: Assessing whether reported results were selected based on the actual results.

Key Signaling Questions:
5.1 Were the data that produced this result analyzed in accordance with a pre-specified analysis plan?
5.2 If N/PN/NI to 5.1: Was the numerical result likely to have been selected on the basis of results?
5.3 If N/PN/NI to 5.1: Was the numerical result likely to have been selected from multiple eligible outcome measurements?
Red Flags for Selective Reporting:
  • No pre-registered protocol or statistical analysis plan
  • Post-hoc subgroup analyses emphasized
  • Unusual statistical methods without justification
  • Time points or scales not pre-specified
  • Multiple testing without adjustment
Overall Risk of Bias Algorithm
Low Risk

Low risk of bias for all domains for this result

Some Concerns

Some concerns in at least one domain for this result, but not high risk of bias for any domain

High Risk

High risk of bias in at least one domain for this result

RoB 2.0 - Risk of Bias in Randomized Trials

Purpose: Assess risk of bias in randomized controlled trials (RCTs) and cluster-randomized trials.
Scientific Background

RoB 2.0, developed by the Cochrane Collaboration, is the revised tool for assessing risk of bias in randomized trials. It replaced the original Cochrane Risk of Bias tool and provides a more structured approach with signaling questions leading to domain-level judgments.

Five Assessment Domains
1. Bias arising from the randomization process

Focus: Sequence generation and allocation concealment

Examines whether the randomization sequence was adequately generated and whether allocation was properly concealed until assignment.

2. Bias due to deviations from intended interventions

Focus: Blinding and adherence to assigned intervention

Assesses deviations from assigned interventions due to the experimental context or deviations that would not occur outside a trial.

3. Bias due to missing outcome data

Focus: Completeness of outcome data and handling of missing data

Evaluates whether missing outcome data could have affected the observed effect estimate.

4. Bias in measurement of the outcome

Focus: Blinding of outcome assessors and measurement methods

Assesses whether outcome measurement was influenced by knowledge of the assigned intervention.

5. Bias in selection of the reported result

Focus: Selective reporting based on results

Examines whether reported results were selected from multiple analyses based on the results.

Judgment Scale
  • Low riskThe study is judged to be at low risk of bias for all domains
  • Some concernsThe study is judged to raise some concerns in at least one domain, but not high risk
  • High riskThe study is judged to be at high risk of bias in at least one domain

ROBINS-I - Risk of Bias In Non-randomized Studies of Interventions

Purpose: Assess risk of bias in non-randomized studies of interventions (cohort studies, case-control studies, controlled before-after studies).
Scientific Background

ROBINS-I evaluates non-randomized studies by comparing them to a hypothetical target randomized trial. It recognizes that non-randomized studies face additional biases that wouldn't occur in randomized trials.

Seven Assessment Domains
Pre-intervention

1. Confounding - Baseline differences between groups

2. Selection of participants - Selection bias related to intervention

At intervention

3. Classification of interventions - Misclassification of intervention status

Post-intervention

4. Deviations from intended interventions - Performance bias

5. Missing data - Attrition bias

6. Measurement of outcomes - Detection bias

7. Selection of reported result - Reporting bias

Key Concept: ROBINS-I assumes that confounding is the most important threat to validity in non-randomized studies. Special attention is given to identifying and assessing potential confounders.

ROBINS-E - Risk of Bias In Non-randomized Studies of Exposure

Purpose: Assess risk of bias in non-randomized studies evaluating the effects of exposures (environmental, occupational, lifestyle factors).
Scientific Background

ROBINS-E is designed for studies where the exposure is not assigned by researchers but occurs naturally. It's particularly relevant for environmental epidemiology and observational studies of harmful exposures.

Key Differences from ROBINS-I
  • Focuses on exposures rather than interventions
  • Considers ethical constraints that prevent randomized trials
  • Addresses time-varying exposures and cumulative effects
  • Emphasizes exposure measurement and classification
Example Applications:
  • Air pollution and respiratory health
  • Dietary factors and disease risk
  • Occupational exposures and cancer
  • Physical activity and cardiovascular outcomes

AMSTAR-2 - Assessment of Multiple Systematic Reviews

Purpose: Assess the methodological quality of systematic reviews that include randomized or non-randomized studies.
Scientific Background

AMSTAR-2 is a critical appraisal tool for systematic reviews. It helps determine the reliability of systematic review findings by evaluating the methods used in conducting the review.

16 Assessment Items
Critical Items (weighted more heavily)
  • Item 2: Protocol registration
  • Item 4: Comprehensive literature search
  • Item 7: List of excluded studies
  • Item 9: Risk of bias assessment
  • Item 11: Appropriate meta-analysis methods
  • Item 13: Impact of risk of bias on results
  • Item 15: Assessment of publication bias
Non-Critical Items
  • Item 1: Research questions and inclusion criteria
  • Item 3: Study design explanation
  • Item 5: Duplicate study selection
  • Item 6: Duplicate data extraction
  • Item 8: Study characteristics description
  • Item 10: Funding sources reporting
  • Item 12: Risk of bias impact assessment
  • Item 14: Heterogeneity discussion
  • Item 16: Conflicts of interest
Overall Confidence Rating
  • HighNo or one non-critical weakness
  • ModerateMore than one non-critical weakness
  • LowOne critical flaw with or without non-critical weaknesses
  • Critically LowMore than one critical flaw with or without non-critical weaknesses

ROBIS - Risk of Bias in Systematic Reviews

Purpose: Assess risk of bias in systematic reviews across all medical and healthcare fields.
Scientific Background

ROBIS is a tool for assessing risk of bias in systematic reviews. Unlike AMSTAR-2 which focuses on methodological quality, ROBIS specifically evaluates bias in the review process.

Three-Phase Assessment
Phase 1: Assess relevance

Determine if the review is relevant to your research question

Phase 2: Identify concerns with the review process
  • Domain 1: Study eligibility criteria
  • Domain 2: Identification and selection of studies
  • Domain 3: Data collection and study appraisal
  • Domain 4: Synthesis and findings
Phase 3: Judge risk of bias in the review

Overall assessment based on phase 2 findings

  • Complete all signaling questions

Interpreting Assessment Results

Understanding Risk of Bias Judgments
Low Risk

Study is unlikely to seriously alter the results

Some Concerns

Study may alter results but unlikely to affect conclusions

High Risk

Study has serious problems that may invalidate results

Reporting Guidelines
  • Always report the specific tool version used
  • Describe how disagreements were resolved
  • Present results visually (traffic light plots)
  • Discuss implications for overall evidence quality
  • Consider sensitivity analyses excluding high-risk studies

Best Practices & Recommendations

Team Assessment
  • Use at least two independent assessors
  • Pilot test the tool on a subset of studies
  • Develop assessment guidelines for your team
  • Calculate inter-rater agreement (kappa)
Documentation
  • Keep detailed justifications for all judgments
  • Record the tool version and date used
  • Document any deviations from standard procedures
  • Maintain an assessment log
Analysis Integration
  • Incorporate bias assessment into evidence synthesis
  • Perform sensitivity analyses
  • Use GRADE to assess evidence certainty
  • Report limitations transparently
Quality Assurance
  • Regular calibration exercises
  • Periodic review of completed assessments
  • Seek expert consultation when uncertain
  • Stay updated with tool revisions
Remember

Risk of bias assessment is not about perfect studies—it's about understanding limitations and their potential impact on results. Transparency and consistency are more important than achieving "low risk" judgments.
